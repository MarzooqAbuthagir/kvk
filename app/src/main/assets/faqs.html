Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)


1. What is climate?

Climate, conditions of the atmosphere at a particular location over a long period of time; it is the long-term summation of the atmospheric elements (and their variations) that, over short time periods, constitute weather. These elements are solar radiation, temperature, humidity, precipitation (type, frequency, and amount), atmospheric pressure, and wind (speed and direction).

2. What is climate change?

Global warming refers to the rise in global temperatures due mainly to the increasing concentrations of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. On the other hand, climate change refers to the increasing changes in the measures of climate over a long period of time – including precipitation, temperature, and wind patterns.

3. How climate change affects agriculture crop production?

Crop production is highly sensitive to climate. It is affected by long-term trends in average rainfall and temperature, interannual climate variability, shocks during specific phenological stages, and extreme weather events. Some crops are more tolerant than others to certain types of stresses, and at each phonological stage, different types of stresses affect each crop species in different ways. A higher concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere will have a small fertilizing effect C3 plants, if all other factors remain favorable in. Increased concentrations of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere will not provide benefits to C4 plants under normal conditions. The increase in average temperature during the growing season typically causes plants to use more energy for respiration for their maintenance and less to support their growth. With a 1°C increase in average temperatures, yields of the major food and cash crop species can decrease by 5 to 10 percent.


Climate change modifies the interactions between plants and their pests in space and over time. Plants weakened by the direct effects of weather stresses are generally more vulnerable to indirect stresses.


The distribution of insect pests is influenced by temperatures. With global warming, insects, whose body temperature varies with the temperature of the surrounding environment are most likely to move polewards and to higher elevations. The extent of crop losses will depend on geographical distribution of insect pests; the dynamics of the insect population; insect biotypes; the alterations in the diversity and abundance of arthropods; changes in herbivore-plant interactions; the activities and abundance of natural enemies; species extinctions; and the efficacy of crop protection technologies.

4. What is climate vulnerability?

It is the degree to which a system is susceptible to, and unable to cope with, adverse effects of climate change, including climate variability and extremes. Vulnerability is a function of the character, magnitude and rate of climate change and variation to which a system is exposed, its sensitivity, and its adaptive capacity.

5. What is drought?

A drought is an event of prolonged shortages in the water supply, whether atmospheric (below-average precipitation), surface water or ground water. Drought is a recurring feature of the climate in most parts of the world. However, these regular droughts have become more extreme and more unpredictable due to climate change.

6. How drought occurs?

Poor or failure of monsoon, high temperature, low relative humidity, fast wind are the reasons for drought.

7. What is climate resilience?

Climate resilience is the ability to anticipate, prepare for, and respond to hazardous events, trends, or disturbances related to climate. Improving climate resilience involves assessing how climate change will create new, or alters current climate-related risks, and taking steps to cope up better with these risks.

8. What is agriculture?

Agriculture is the art and science of cultivating the soil, growing crops and raising livestock.

9. What is climate-resilient agriculture (CRA)?

Climate-resilient agriculture (CRA) is an approach that includes sustainably using existing natural resources through crop and livestock production systems to achieve long-term higher productivity and farm income under different climate variability.

10. What are the general management practices to be followed to overcome the adverse effect of drought?

  1. Rain water harvesting structures - community ponds, percolation ponds, check dams, farm ponds and rain water harvesting unit at institution and household will effectively harvest and store rain water
  2. Recycling of waste water-Construction of waste water recycling unit at all premises to irrigate crops in surrounding areas conserve water in better way
  3. Micro irrigation system-Devices like drip irrigation, sprinkler, rain gun, laser spray and sub surface can be adopted to minimize the water loss in surface irrigation
  4. Crop rotation – growing different types of crops in every season helps to improve soil structure and water holding capacity

11. Is there any measures for insitu moisture conservation to overcome the adverse effect of drought?

  1. Summer ploughing – Normally carried out during summer months to break the hard crusted upper layer of soil and helps to retain moisture in soil
  2. Conservation tillage/No tillage – Protect the soil surface from wind, sun and heavy rain
  3. Deep tillage – suited for some areas and soils, deep tillage can increase porosity and permeability of the soil to increase its water absorption capacity
  4. Compartmental bunding - Small compartments act as a dam and store rain water received in the compartments for longer period
  5. Contour bunding and trenches – Forming bunds and trenches across the slope conserve the water during the monsoon
  6. Spreading manure or compost over the soil - Minimizes evapotranspiration and also provides valuable nutrients to the soil through processes of decomposition
  7. Mulching - Mulch is a layer of organic (or inorganic) material that is placed on the root zone of the plants

12. What are the contingency measures to mitigate drought in crops?

  • Imbibe seeds, setts and rhizome with 0.1 % PPFM (Pink pigmented facultative methylotrophs) or 0.5% KCL (potassium chloride) for 20 minutes to induce tolerance of seed materials during establishment stage
  • Foliar spraying with 0.1 % PPFM -200ml /acre/spray to induce tolerance of crops during the critical growth stages
  • Foliar spray of 2% DAP + 1% KCl (MOP) during critical stages of flowering and grain formation
  • 3% Kaoline spray at critical stages of moisture stress
  • Foliar spray of 500 ppm Cycocel (1 ml of commercial product per litre of water)
  • Mulching with 5 tones of sorghum / sugarcane trash, which saves 19-20% of irrigation water by reducing evaporation loss of water
  • Split application of N and K fertilizers as in cotton at 45 and 60 DAS
  • Use of biofertilizers viz., Azospirillum or phosphobacteria @ 10 packets / ha along with 25 kg of soil or FYM
  • Application of 12.5 kg / ha along with 37.5 kg of sand
  • Seed hardening with 1% KH2PO4 and other salts for 6 – 8 hours (depending upon nature of seed coat) soaked in equal volume of water
  • Spray of 40 ppm NAA (4 ml of Planofix in 4.5 litres of water)
  • Seed treatment + soil application + foliar spray of Pink Pigmented Facultative Methnaotrops (PPFM) @ 106 as a source of cytokinins.
  • As in cotton, nipping terminal portion f main stem beyond 15th (at 70 - 80 DAS) and at 20th node (at 90 DAS) in the case of hybrids and varieties respectively for arresting transpiratory loss of water)
  • Foliar spray of 0.5% zinc sulphate + 0.3 % boric acid + 0.5 % Ferrous sulphate + 1% urea during critical stages of moisture stress
  • Spraying should be done during the morning or evening hours
  • It can be recommended to all crops
  • Spraying should be done at critical growth stages of crops at 15 to 20 days intervals
  • Precautions: Do not mix with pesticide/fungicide and fertilizers while spraying
  • Drought resistant, short duration, high yielding varieties may be selected and cultivated
  • Selection of crops – Low water requirement plants may be encouraged with all mitigation measures.

14. What is water stress?

The availability of water to the plants depends on the periodicity, degree of rainfall, sub-soil water status, water slope etc. Water stress may occur either due to water shortage or due to excess of water. The former is more common in India and in arid and semi-arid regions and causes Drought, while the latter is caused by the Flood and Water logging resulting in O2 insufficiency to the roots.

15. What is flooding (High moisture stress)?

Water logging refers to a condition when water is present in excess amount than its optimum requirement. It creates an anaerobic situation in the rhizosphere due to which the plant experiences the stress (O2 deficient stress).

16. What can be done to prevent water logging in fields?

  • Providing adequate drainage for draining excessive stagnating water around the root system
  • Spray of growth retardant of 500 ppm cycocel for arresting apical dominance and thereby promoting growth of laterals
  • Foliar spray of 2% DAP + 1% KCl (MOP)
  • Nipping terminal buds for arresting apical dominance and thus promoting growth sympodial branches (as in cotton) for increasing productivity
  • Spray of 40 ppm NAA for controlling excessive pre-mature fall of flowering/buds/young developing fruits and pods
  • Spray of 0.5 ppm brassinolide for increasing photosynthetic activity
  • Foliar spray of 100 ppm salicylic acid for increasing stem reserve utilization under high moisture stress
  • Foliar spray of 0.3 % Boric acid + 0.5 % ZnSO4 + 0.5 % FeSO4 + 1.0 % urea during critical stages of the stress

17. What is temperature Stress?

Plant life exists between temperatures of -89OC to +58OC . However most of the plants are adapted to limited range of temperature. If the temperature drops below 15OC, plants experienced to low temperature stress and if it is above 45OC , plants are subjected to high temperature stress. Increase of 15-20OC above normal temperature causes deeper modification of growth without being necessarily lethal viz., protein denaturation, enzyme inactivation, reduction in chloroplast’s photosynthetic activity. Temperature stress includes both high temperature and low temperature stresses.

18. How wind plays a role in crops growth?

The direction and velocity have significant influence on crop growth. Wind increases the turbulence in atmosphere, thus increasing the supply of carbon dioxide to the plants resulting in greater photosynthesis rates.

19. How to measure the weather status of a particular place?

To predict the correct weather, a weather station may be established with following instruments


  1. Thermometer for measuring air temperature
  2. Rain gauge for measuring liquid precipitation over a set period of time
  3. Hydrometer for measuring humidity
  4. Anemometer for measuring wind speed
  5. Wind vane for measuring wind blowing direction

20. How to know the weather status of particular district?

IMD has set up in India a network of about 130 Agro Meteorological Field Units (AMFUs) which are multidisciplinary units responsible for preparation and dissemination of district and sub-district agromet advisories. These AMFUs are located at State Agricultural Universities, ICAR centres and other institutions. Each AMFU utilizes the relevant output products including weather data from conventional/ automatic weather station (AWS) provided by IMD and ICAR to generate specific advisories for agricultural management for the respective districts of Agro-climatic zones identified under the area of its jurisdiction and disseminate the same to the farming community. Under the Gramin Krishi Mausam Sewa, the IMD proposes to establish District AgroMet Units (DAMU) in 530 districts, in addition to already operating 130 AMFUs, in order to meet the said expansion. Among other responsibilities, DAMU will receive weather forecast from IMD to prepare and disseminate sub-district level agromet advisory bulletins.


In Namakkal district Agro Meteorological Field Unit (AMFU) is functioning at Veterinary College and Research Institute Campus and weekly weather forecast circulated to farmers on Tuesday and Friday. One manual weather station also established at KVK, Namakkal

21. What is protected agriculture?

It is a process of growing crops in a controlled environment. This means that the temperature, humidity, light and such other factors can be regulated as per requirement of the crop. This assists in a healthier and a larger produce. There are various types of protected cultivation practices.


Mostly vegetables, greens, flower crops, sugarcane setts under SSI method can be grown under this method.

22. How Integrated Farming System (IFS) supports in farmers income under drought situation?

In case of crop failure or economic loss in one enterprise it can be met out from the income of other enterprises while adopting IFS components in farmers fields.


Components of Integrated Farming System


  1. Crops, livestock, birds and trees are the major components of any IFS.
  2. Crop may have subsystem like monocrop, mixed/intercrop, multi-tier crops of cereals, legumes (pulses), oilseeds, forage etc.
  3. Livestock components may be milch cow, goat, sheep, poultry, bees.
  4. Tree components may include timer, fuel, fodder and fruit trees.

23. What are the factors to be considered in rainfed IFS?

Soil types, rainfall and its distribution and length of growing season are the major factors that decide the selection of suitable annual crops, trees and livestock components. The needs and resource base of the farmers also decides the selection of IFS components in any farm.


  • Adoption of improved cropping system according to the rainfall and soil moisture availability
  • Selection of suitable grain crop species, tree species that supply pods/leaves for a longer period or throughout the year
  • The surplus fodder leaves, crop residues etc. during the rainy season should be preserved as silage/hay for lean season (summer)

24. What are major diseases affecting animals in Namakkal District?

Food and Mouth disease in cattle, Enterotoxemia and blue tongue in sheep, Peste Des Petis Ruminants (PPR) in sheep and goats and Ranikhet Disease in poultry are the common diseases affecting animals and desi chicken.
